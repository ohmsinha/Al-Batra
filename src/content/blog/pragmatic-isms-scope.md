Professional services firms in the UAE — law, advisory, accounting, financial services, IT — are increasingly asked for ISO 27001 by clients, regulators and tender procurement teams. The temptation is to maximise scope: include every system, every controller, every Annex A control. The result is a documentation library a firm of fifty cannot maintain.

A pragmatic ISMS for a firm under one hundred staff looks different.

## Scope the boundary, not the firm

ISO 27001 lets you define the ISMS boundary. For a small firm, the boundary is usually the practice that handles the regulated or sensitive data — not necessarily the entire firm. A law firm's litigation practice can hold the certificate without dragging conveyancing into scope. A consultancy's regulated advisory line can hold it without including the marketing team.

## Statement of Applicability — exclusions are not failures

A Statement of Applicability that excludes Annex A controls with a documented justification is stronger than one that includes everything badly. Auditors expect to see exclusions where they make sense.

## Risk treatment — three real risks, well controlled, beat thirty paper risks

A small firm's risk register should contain the risks the firm actually faces — typically a handful of credible scenarios. Each one needs a documented treatment, an owner, and an evidence trail.

## Internal audit — nominate one auditor, train them properly

A small firm does not need an audit team. One nominated internal auditor, properly trained and supported, is enough.

## Management review — quarterly is plenty

For a small firm, a quarterly management review meeting with the standard inputs and outputs is plenty. Monthly reviews tend to become rubber-stamping exercises that auditors discount.

The result of all this restraint is an ISMS the firm can actually operate.
